Frequently Asked Questions

A normal blood pressure reading is typically around 120/80 mmHg or lower. According to the American Heart Association:

  • Normal: Less than 120/80 mmHg
  • Elevated: 120-129/less than 80 mmHg
  • Stage 1 Hypertension: 130-139/80-89 mmHg
  • Stage 2 Hypertension: 140+/90+ mmHg
  • Hypertensive Crisis: 180+/120+ mmHg (seek immediate medical attention)

The frequency depends on your health status:

  • Normal readings: Check every 1-2 years if you have no risk factors
  • Elevated readings: Check at least once a month
  • Hypertension: As directed by your doctor (often daily or weekly)
  • New medication: May need to check multiple times per day initially

For the most accurate tracking, take readings at the same time each day, preferably in the morning before medications and in the evening before bed.

Many temporary factors can influence your blood pressure:

Common Elevators

  • Stress or anxiety
  • Caffeine consumption
  • Recent exercise
  • Full bladder
  • Nicotine use

Common Reducers

  • Deep breathing exercises
  • Proper hydration
  • Regular physical activity
  • Meditation/relaxation
  • Medications

This tool provides general information based on standard blood pressure classification guidelines from the American Heart Association. However, there are important limitations:

  • It cannot replace professional medical advice
  • Accuracy depends on correct input of your readings
  • Individual health factors may affect interpretation
  • Doesn't account for medications or specific conditions

Always consult with your healthcare provider about your blood pressure readings and what they mean for your specific health situation.

If you get a high reading:

  1. Stay calm - anxiety can raise blood pressure further
  2. Retake after 5 minutes of quiet sitting
  3. Check your technique (arm position, cuff placement)
  4. Track multiple readings over time
  5. Contact your doctor if consistently high

Seek immediate medical attention if:

  • Reading is 180/120 mmHg or higher
  • You experience severe headache, chest pain, or vision changes
  • You have difficulty breathing or neurological symptoms
